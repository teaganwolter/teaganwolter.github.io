Teaching and Lectures

Taught Courses

Introduction to the Archaeology of the Ancient Near East 1: Mesopotamia

This course is periodically taught as one of UChicago’s Department of Near Eastern Languages and Civilizations’ introductory archaeology sequence. As the instructor for this course, I created my own syllabus for the course, including selecting readings and creating assignments that would be appropriate for both graduate and undergraduate students. I also created my own lectures and arranged a guest lecturer for one session.

Humanities Writing Seminars

Humanities Writing Seminars are courses taught alongside UChicao’s Humanities Core classes, which are required for all first-year students. I was responsible for creating my own writing curriculum for the students, delivered in the form of two small-group seminars and one round of one-on-one meetings for each section. Small-group seminars consisted of a short lecture on a writing topic, along with examples of techniques to help students critique their own writing. This would be followed by directed peer critique of a sample of writing provided by each student. The final one-on-one meetings were less structured and catered to each student’s needs.

Lectures

Unconvering a Lost Civilization: Archaeology and the Bronze Age of Central Asia

This public lecture, hosted by UChicago's Center for East European and Russian/Eurasian Center and the Barrington Area Public Library, discussed the process of archaeological discovery through the example of the Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex. The lecture can be viewed on YouTube here.

The Neolithic Revolution: Precursors, Process, and Aftermath

I was invited to give a guest lecture for the class Introduction to the Middle East. My lecture served as a general overview of the origins of sedentism and agriculture in the ancient Middle East.
